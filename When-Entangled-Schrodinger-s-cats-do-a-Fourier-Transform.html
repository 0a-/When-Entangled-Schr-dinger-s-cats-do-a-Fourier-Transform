A qubit is a schrödinger's cat.

Can be both 1 and 0 at the same time


A classical bit is classical.

Is either 1 or 0.

Qubits are entangled.

A $n$ qubits system can be expressed as
a sum of all possible states,
each multiplying a probability amplitude

$$\sum^{2^n-1}_{i=0} a_i \left |S_i \right\rangle \\\begin{align} &\text{ where } \left |S_i \right\rangle \text{ is one of the $2^n$ possible states, can be expressed as }\sum^{n-1}_{a=0} 2^a d_a \\ &a_i \text{ is a complex number and } a{_i}^2 \text{ is the possibility of being in that state} \end{align}$$

Classical bits are classical.

$$\sum^{n-1}_{a=0} 2^a d_a \text{ where } d_a \text{ is 0 or 1 (the boolean value of a-th digit)}$$

a system with 1 qubit

$$a \left | 0 \right\rangle + b \left | 1 \right\rangle $$
$$\text{where } a^2+b^2=1$$

a system with 3 qubits

$$\begin{align} &a \left | 000 \right\rangle + b \left | 001 \right\rangle + c \left | 010 \right\rangle + d \left | 011 \right\rangle + \\ &e \left | 100 \right\rangle + f \left | 101 \right\rangle + g \left | 110 \right\rangle + h \left | 111 \right\rangle \end{align}$$
$$\text{where } a^2+b^2+c^2+d^2+e^2+f^2+g^2+h^2 = 1$$

Entangled cats.

Each bit can thus be expressed
as a $n^2$-dimensional vector

$$\text{one qubit, }a \left | 0 \right\rangle + b \left | 1 \right\rangle \text{ , can be expressed as } \begin{pmatrix} a\\ b \end{pmatrix}$$

Each quantum gate can thus be seen as a unitary matrix. (hence "reversible" in chapter 3)

$$\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1\\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} a\\ b \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} b\\ a \end{pmatrix}$$

manipulating qubits == doing linear algebra

vs

manipulating classical bits == doing boolean algebra

Quantum Fourier Transform

It's basically the quantum implementation of the Discrete Fourier transform.

$$\frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 &... & 1 \\ 1 & ω & ω^2 & ω^3 & ... & ω^{N-1} \\ 1 & ω^2 & w^4 & ω^6 & ... & w^{2(N-1)} \\ . & . & . & . & . & . \\ 1 & ω^{N-1} & ω^{2(N-1)} & ω^{3(N-1)} & ... & ω^{(N-1)^2} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \alpha_1 \\ \alpha_2 \\ \alpha_3 \\ . \\ \alpha_N \end{pmatrix} $$
where $ω = e^{ (2 \pi i)/N}$ and $N$ is the number of possible states ($2^n$ for $n$-qubits)

Let there be a function $f:{0,1,...M} \Rightarrow S$, such that for all $x$, $f(x) = f(X+r)$.


Shor shows that we can implement a quantum circuit with QFT that can help us find r much faster comparing to classical approach.
